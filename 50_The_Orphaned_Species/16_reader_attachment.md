# Reader Attachment

*Craft guide for making readers care about the characters before asking them to carry the cosmology. Companion to `05_story_architecture.md` and `15_character_grounding_and_relatability.md`.*

## Governing rule

Make readers attached before asking them to understand the mythology.

The Anunnaki, fossil record, stone circles, artifacts, and hidden history create scale. They do not create attachment by themselves. Attachment comes from watching specific people want ordinary things and get hurt trying to keep them.

Every major character needs three page-level anchors:

1. **A small human need** — something ordinary the reader can understand immediately.
2. **A private wound** — the fear or shame that distorts their choices.
3. **A repeated behavior** — a recognizable action that becomes familiar before it becomes meaningful.

If a scene loses those anchors, the character becomes a carrier of architecture rather than a person.

## Eli's attachment engine

Eli does not need to be made generic. He needs to be made precise.

Readers attach to him through concrete wants:

- he wants a home he does not have to earn every day;
- he wants to know what happened to his mother;
- he wants his body to stop betraying him;
- he wants to be useful without being owned;
- he wants someone to see him before they interpret him.

The cosmic material matters only because it threatens these things. If a revelation does not endanger home, body, belonging, trust, ordinary work, love, or self-command, it is probably arriving too early or too abstractly.

## Introduce people through care, not lore

Before a character becomes important to the mythos, show them in ordinary conduct.

For Eli, that means work shifts, fatigue, careful money, ferry schedules, food habits, sailing routines, quiet adaptations to brain injury, and the small social intelligence of someone trying not to be a burden.

For other characters, the same rule applies: show what they maintain before showing what they know.

- Wren should be seen building exits, checking routes, or protecting someone else's options before she explains systems.
- The healer should be seen doing ordinary medicine, washing, triaging, refusing, and getting tired before miracle pressure gathers around him.
- The Pai staff-twirler should be seen performing because play and beauty matter, not because the dragon image foreshadows a symbol.
- The Forest City martial-arts teacher should open morning practice whether five people come or no one does.
- Crane should believe fear is the only honest political force before he becomes an antagonist label.

Readers attach when a character has a life before the plot eats it.

## Repeated ordinary anchors

Give each major character a repeated behavior the reader can recognize.

Possible anchors:

| Character | Repeated behavior |
|---|---|
| Eli | Repairs things carefully; memorizes ferry or boat schedules; keeps a cheap meal ritual; touches metal railings when anxious; writes practical lists. |
| Wren | Carries too many backup plans; checks exits; fixes other people's routes before admitting she needs one. |
| Healer | Washes his hands too long; checks on patients after hours; falls asleep at meals; says no before he can explain why. |
| Pai staff-twirler | Jokes when frightened; keeps practicing even after humiliation; performs because beauty is not optional during crisis. |
| Forest City teacher | Begins morning training on time; corrects footwork before philosophy; treats restraint as a skill. |
| Emrys | Overtrusts maps, notes, and old systems; reorganizes evidence when afraid; fears meaninglessness more than danger. |
| Crane | Reduces fear to policy; interprets every unregulated bond as a future disaster. |

These anchors should recur under changed pressure. Familiarity creates attachment; changed context creates development.

## Sympathetic wrongness

Perfect characters are hard to love. Give each major character mistakes that arise from understandable needs.

- Eli hides information because he fears belonging will be revoked.
- Wren controls situations because she has seen unregulated power destroy people.
- The healer refuses public worship but may also refuse intimacy.
- Emrys overtrusts maps, texts, and old systems because he fears meaningless suffering.
- The Pai staff-twirler treats danger as theater until theater fails to protect someone.
- Crane's best argument is that frightened societies really do hurt people when power is unaccountable.

Readers care when flaws create consequences and when the flaw is human before it is thematic.

## Relationships must change through action

Do not ask declarations to carry attachment. Make choices do it.

Examples:

- Wren risks her objective to protect Eli, then resents the cost.
- Eli tells the truth when lying would keep him safer.
- The healer refuses a miracle and loses public love.
- A rival psion spares someone because they recognize the same fear.
- The martial-arts teacher teaches Eli one ordinary physical habit that later saves him.

After such a choice, the relationship should not reset. The reader should be able to say: they are different with each other now because of what happened.

## Warmth before catastrophe

If the reader has not seen breakfast, jokes, irritation, embarrassment, work, boredom, affection, fatigue, and small repair, the disasters will feel abstract.

For this trilogy, ordinary scenes are not filler. They are the moral payload. The ending says ordinary life is the reward, so the beginning and middle must make ordinary life lovable.

Use warmth before catastrophe in every volume:

- meals before pursuit;
- work before displacement;
- practice before combat;
- ordinary medicine before miracle;
- performance before omen;
- local duties before global stakes;
- private joking before public interpretation.

## Emotional questions by volume

Each volume needs a human emotional question underneath the external objective.

| Volume | Emotional question |
|---|---|
| **I — Shattering** | Can Eli stay himself long enough to find one place where he is not treated as evidence, weapon, or mistake? |
| **II — Descent** | Can these people love, heal, and trust each other without turning care into control? |
| **III — Choice** | Can a community survive contact with power without kneeling, killing, owning, or worshipping? |

These questions should be legible even if a reader ignores most of the mythological architecture.

## Make loss specific

Do not only threaten "humanity." Threaten things with names, textures, routines, and witnesses.

Specific losses can include:

- the boat Eli learned on;
- the kitchen where people stopped treating him as a guest;
- the healer's right to eat one meal in peace;
- Wren's chance to save someone without becoming Crane;
- the staff-twirler's ability to perform without being called an omen;
- a child, patient, coworker, teacher, neighbor, field, route, or room the reader has already inhabited.

Scale becomes emotional only when it has a face.

## Scene test

For every chapter, ask:

> If all mythological exposition were removed, would the reader still care because of what these people want from each other?

If yes, the scene has life.

If no, the scene is probably carrying architecture instead of story.

## Drafting checklist

Before drafting or keeping a scene, answer:

1. Which character does the reader become more attached to here?
2. What ordinary want, wound, or repeated behavior is visible?
3. What does this character risk that is not abstract?
4. What warmth, humor, care, or irritation makes the person specific?
5. How does one relationship change through action?
6. What could be lost that the reader has actually seen?
7. If the lore were removed, what human problem would remain?

If a scene cannot answer at least three of these, revise it before adding more cosmology.
