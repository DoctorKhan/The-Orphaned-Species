## Chapter One — Lines in the Sand

At dawn the island belongs to the ones who work before anyone photographs it.

The beach is empty in the blue hour, or nearly empty: two men dragging a net above the tide-line, a woman sweeping last night's cups from the sand, a dog asleep beneath the ribs of an overturned boat. Farther down, where the hotels have not yet opened their white umbrellas and the music has not yet begun, Eli stands barefoot with a stick in each hand and waits for the old man to try to cut him.

The old man is not old in the way Americans mean it. He is narrow and weathered and small enough that tourists step around him without seeing him, but when he moves, the morning makes room. His name is Jun, though everyone on the beach calls him Mang Jun, and he has spent three weeks teaching Eli that a blade is mostly a line.

"Again," Jun says.

Eli raises the sticks.

The first strike comes from the right, clean and diagonal, shoulder to hip. Eli meets it and turns, left foot cutting back through the sand, right shoulder closing the line before the second strike can open him. Jun's other stick flashes low. Eli sees it late. He has seen it late every morning. The rattan catches him across the thigh hard enough to light the nerve.

"Dead," Jun says.

Eli exhales through his teeth and steps back.

"Again."

The tide keeps its own count. In and out. In and out. Eli's breath tries to follow it, but pain has made him stupid, and the thing about pain is that it makes every man want to become a fist. He knows this about himself. He knows most things about himself first in the body and years later, if ever, in words.

He resets his feet.

Boracay behind him is beginning to wake into its lie. Engines turning over. Staff hosing salt from the walkways. A blender coughing once in a closed bar. By eight the same sand will be full of honeymooners and phones and women in bright coverups stepping carefully around the poorer parts of paradise. By noon the sea will be a thousand small performances of freedom. But now, before the island remembers what it sells, there is only water and breath and the old man across from him, holding two pieces of rattan as if they are the plainest truth in the world.

"You think too much," Jun says.

Eli almost laughs. It would be the first time in days.

"Everybody tells me that."

"No." Jun taps the side of his own head with one stick. "They mean you talk inside. I mean you leave."

That stops him.

Jun studies him with the unsentimental patience of a man inspecting a knot in rope. "When you are hit, you go somewhere. When you are afraid, you go somewhere. I can see the body stay. But you go."

Eli looks down at the line his foot has carved in the damp sand. The water reaches it, brightens it, takes it away.

"Again," Jun says, softer.

They move.

This time Eli does not try to win the exchange. Winning makes him loud. He lets the first line come. Lets his own body answer before the thought can stand up and ruin it. Right hand checks. Left hand cuts. Foot turns. Breath goes out. Jun's low strike starts, and Eli is already gone from where it was meant to land, not escaped, not panicked, simply elsewhere by the width of a shin.

Jun's stick stops a finger from his ribs.

For a moment neither of them moves.

"There," Jun says.

The word lands in Eli's chest before it lands in his ear.

There.

It happens sometimes. A word, a face, a turn in a song. No warning, no pattern he has ever been able to read. A heat begins behind the breastbone and moves outward in a ring, up the throat and into the scalp, until the fine hairs lift along his arms. It is not pleasure. It is not fear. It is certainty, and that is worse, because certainty ought to arrive with something to be certain of.

He lowers the sticks.

Jun frowns. "You are hurt?"

"No."

"Then why you look like that?"

Because a man he met three weeks ago has just said a word that opened a door in him for half a second, and behind the door there was dust, and a fox cut in stone, and a hand not his own holding a tool to the eye of it.

Because the same thing happened yesterday when a child ran past the training space humming four notes under her breath.

Because two nights ago, drunk and sleepless in the room above the dive shop, he woke with his palms hot enough to fog the window glass from a foot away.

Because in the bottom pocket of his bag is a folded printout from an adoption agency in Oregon, and on it are two names that have begun, somehow, to pull at the inside of his bones.

He says none of this.

"Tired," Eli says.

Jun looks unconvinced, which is fair. Eli has been tired for most of his life and has rarely looked like that.

They finish when the sun clears the water. Jun leaves him with bruises on both arms, one split knuckle, and the first clean entry Eli has managed since arriving on the island. This is Jun's method of praise: injury, correction, silence.

"Tomorrow," Jun says.

"I leave tomorrow."

Jun's face changes only a little. "Bangladesh."

Eli nods.

The old man squints toward the brightening sea. "For family."

It is not a question. Jun does not waste questions on things a body has already said.

"Maybe," Eli says.

"Maybe is not family."

"No."

"Then why go?"

Eli wraps the sticks in the faded towel Jun lent him the first day and has refused to take back. "Because someone wrote down a name. Because I asked the wrong office enough times and they got tired. Because my adoption file has a father in it now, or half of one. Because there is an aunt in Dhaka who answered one email and then stopped answering the rest."

Jun waits.

"Because I don't know what else to do with it."

This, apparently, is acceptable. Jun nods once.

"When you find your father, do not stand square."

Eli looks at him.

"People hurt you on the centerline," Jun says. "Family most of all."

The old man walks away before Eli can decide whether to thank him.

By then the island has opened its eyes. Music begins somewhere behind him, soft at first and then insistent. A young couple in linen poses at the tide-line while a photographer crouches in the water to make them look alone in paradise. Men roll paddleboards down the beach. A vendor lights charcoal with a square of cardboard and patient breath.

Eli sits beneath a palm and peels the tape from his knuckles.

The file in his bag is thin. Too thin for a life. An American adoption certificate. A hospital intake sheet with the left margin cut off by a bad scan. The Oregon agency's release form. Two names in an email from a clerk who had written as if names were small things.

Mother: Elianor Vale. Nationality: British.

Father: Arif Rahman. Nationality: Bangladeshi.

There should have been more. There was not.

He had read the names first in a laundromat in Manila while rain hammered the plastic roof and a woman argued with a dryer. He had expected nothing from them. The adoption people had warned him, gently and repeatedly, not to expect anything from them. Names were not parents. Names were not answers. Names were only sounds the state had failed to lose.

But when he read them, the heat had come. Not the little uncertain flicker he sometimes got from words in songs. This was stronger. It moved through him so hard he had gripped the edge of the folding table until the metal bent under his fingers, and for one instant the laundromat had vanished and he had smelled wet earth, smoke, salt, old paper, and something green rotting sweetly in heat.

Then the dryers had clunked on, and the woman had kept arguing, and he was still an American with no family and a bag of damp clothes.

He folds the tape into a small gray knot and puts it in his pocket because Jun hates litter.

His phone buzzes.

For a second he thinks it is the airline. The flight to Manila, then Kuala Lumpur, then Dhaka. The route was cheaper than the direct ones and humiliatingly fragile, but it existed, and existing was most of what Eli asked of plans.

The message is not from the airline.

It is from an address he does not know.

Subject: Re: Rahman family inquiry

The body knows before the mind. Heat under the breastbone. Salt air gone suddenly cold. He opens it with hands that, by rights, should be shaking and are not.

Mr. Hart,

Do not come through the airport records desk. Do not ask for Arif Rahman by name again. If you are already in transit, continue. If anyone asks, you are visiting for martial arts tourism and onward travel.

Your father was not the dangerous one.

Your mother was.

There is an attachment. A photograph, badly lit, probably scanned from paper. Three people standing beside a river in a city Eli does not recognize. A young Bangladeshi man with one hand lifted against the sun. A woman beside him, pale, dark-haired, unsmiling, looking not at the camera but just beyond it, as if she has heard someone call her from very far away.

And behind them, half cut off by the edge of the photograph, an older man in a linen jacket.

Eli does not know him.

But the heat in his chest does.

The old man in the photograph is standing with one hand at his side, palm open, as though feeling for rain.

Eli zooms in until the picture breaks into squares.

There, pinned to the old man's jacket, just visible in the ruined scan, is a printed conference badge.

G. EMRYS

CONSCIOUSNESS & THRESHOLD STUDIES

The sea keeps coming in. The island keeps pretending. Down the beach, Jun lifts one hand without turning, as if he has felt the change in the air.

Eli sits very still beneath the palm, the phone hot in his hands, and understands with the old useless certainty that his trip has already begun to turn into something else.

## Chapter Two — The Aunt Who Would Not Say His Name

Dhaka receives him in heat, noise, and a sky the color of old metal.

The airport has the exhausted brightness of places built to move people through them faster than fear can gather. Families wait behind railings. Men hold signs. A child sleeps across two suitcases with her mouth open and one shoe missing. Above it all the screens flicker between arrivals, delays, advertisements for banks, and a government notice about regional security cooperation that no one appears to read.

Eli reads it because he reads exits, notices, guards, cameras, shoes, hands. Kali has made nothing new in him. It has only given names to habits he earned badly.

He keeps his passport in his front pocket and the printed adoption file flat against his ribs beneath his shirt. The file is damp already. Everything is damp already. Oregon paper was not made for this country.

At immigration the officer looks from the passport to Eli's face.

"United States."

"Yes."

"Purpose."

The answer Jun gave him comes back with the dry authority of a stick across the thigh.

"Tourism. Family visit. Training."

"Training?"

"Martial arts. Philippines first. Now here."

The officer looks bored, which is the only blessing a border can give. He stamps the passport and slides it back.

Outside, the air hits like wet cloth.

His aunt is not his aunt by any definition the adoption agency would respect. She is his father's cousin, or possibly his father's half-sister; the first email had said one thing and the second, months later, had said another. Her name is Farida. She is smaller than he expected and older than the photograph she sent, with silver hair pinned severely at the back of her head and a phone in one hand that she does not put away even when she embraces him.

The embrace is brief and fiercely practical, as if she is checking that he is real.

"You look American," she says.

"I am American."

"No," she says, and studies him again. "That is not what I mean."

He has no answer to that. He has spent his life with no answer to that.

They ride in a small car through a city that seems to have no still surface. Buses painted like festival animals. Rickshaws shining in impossible colors. Cables sagging from pole to pole. Water standing black in broken places beside the road. The driver uses the horn the way other men use language, and Farida speaks over it without raising her voice.

"You will stay one night. Maybe two. Then you go."

Eli turns from the window. "I just got here."

"Yes. That is why I say it now."

"Is someone watching you?"

Farida looks at him then, sharply.

There it is. The same small shift he has seen in fighters when the line changes. Not fear exactly. Recognition of range.

"Who taught you to ask like that?"

"People who lied badly."

For the first time, almost, she smiles.

"Your father also answered questions with knives."

Eli feels the sentence enter him and fail to find a place to stand.

Your father.

He has said the phrase himself, alone, like testing a word in a foreign language. From another person it is different. It has weight. It asks something of the room.

"What was he like?"

"Too kind for his own good. Too proud when kindness failed. Good with engines. Bad with money. Loved rivers. Hated offices. Wrote letters he did not send." Farida looks out the window. "Loved your mother more than was wise."

Eli waits.

"That is all for the car," she says.

Her apartment is on the fourth floor of a narrow building above a pharmacy. The stairwell smells of dust, frying oil, damp concrete, and something medicinal. On the landing outside her door, Farida stops and takes his wrist.

Not hard. Not dramatically. Just enough to halt him.

"Inside, you do not say her name."

"My mother's."

"Any of them."

"How many names did she have?"

Farida's mouth tightens. "You see? American. You think a question is innocent because you want the answer."

Inside the apartment, a ceiling fan turns with a tired clicking sound. There are books in Bengali and English, a framed photograph of a young man Eli recognizes from the river picture, and on a shelf above the television a brass bowl filled with keys. Farida takes his shoes before she takes his bag. She gives him water. She watches until he drinks all of it.

"You are thinner than Arif was," she says. "But the hands are hers."

Eli looks down.

The glass has fogged where his fingers hold it. Not from the room. From him.

Farida sees. Of course she sees. The apartment changes around the seeing.

Until that moment it had been an old woman's apartment in a hot city, full of ordinary objects arranged by long habit. Now the fan seems louder. The street below falls back. Even the keys in the brass bowl look suddenly less like keys than evidence.

Farida takes the glass from him and sets it in the sink.

"How long?"

"Always."

"Always is not an answer."

"Since I can remember."

"And the words?"

He says nothing.

"Yes," she says. "The words also."

She goes to the shelf and takes down the brass bowl. Under the keys is a folded cloth, and inside the cloth is a small envelope gone soft at the corners. She does not hand it to him immediately.

"Your father gave this to me before he left," she says. "He said if a boy ever came with his face and her hands, I should give it to him. I told him he was being dramatic. He said drama is what people call a pattern before they understand it."

Eli cannot breathe properly.

Farida gives him the envelope.

There is no name on it. Only a mark drawn in black ink: a fox's head in profile, and in the eye, a small spiral.

The heat comes so fast he nearly drops it.

Farida steps back.

"Ah," she says quietly. "So it is true."

The room tilts. Not much. Enough.

For half a second he is not in Dhaka. He is kneeling in dust with a tool in his hand and the smell of crushed limestone in his mouth. A child is watching him from beside a basket. He wants to tell her something and cannot remember what language the living use.

Then he is back, one hand on Farida's table, the envelope bent under his fingers.

Farida has gone very pale.

"Sit down," she says.

He sits because the alternative is falling.

"There was a man," she says. "English. Older. Emrys. Your mother trusted him, I think. Your father did not. Before you were born, before the adoption, before all the papers became wrong, they came here once. Your mother was already afraid. Not of pregnancy. Not of scandal. Of being found."

"By who?"

Farida looks at the door.

No one has knocked. No one is there.

But Eli's hands have gone cold.

"By the people who make records true," she says.

The lights flicker once. The fan slows, catches, resumes.

Somewhere outside, far off or very near, a siren begins.

Farida reaches for the envelope in his hand and closes his fingers around it.

"You should not have come through the airport records desk," she says.

"I didn't."

"Then someone taught you one useful thing."

His phone buzzes in his pocket. Once. Twice. Then every phone in the apartment starts at the same time: Farida's, the driver's old handset left charging by the door, the little emergency phone on the shelf. All of them speaking in different tones, all of them receiving the same message.

The language on Eli's screen is English.

REGIONAL SECURITY NOTICE. COMMUNICATIONS DISRUPTION EXPECTED. REMAIN IN PLACE. AWAIT OFFICIAL GUIDANCE.

The signal bars vanish while he is reading.

Farida looks at her dead phone, then at Eli, then at the envelope marked with the fox.

"Official guidance," she says, and there is no humor in it. "Now we are in trouble."

Below them, the city changes pitch.

Not panic. Not yet. Only the first turn of a very large lock.
