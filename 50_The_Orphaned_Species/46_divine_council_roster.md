# The Divine Council — a reconstructed roster

*Working roster of the "seventy sons of El" / the gods of the nations, for Book 5. This is the partial, defensible reconstruction described in `45_divine_names.md` — assembled from the four evidence streams, with confidence flagged. It is grounding for the bible, not prose, and not a decoder ring (see craft rules at the end and in `45_divine_names.md`).*

---

## Read this first — the honest caveats

1. **There is no ancient master-list of seventy named sons.** "70" is a symbolic, recurring slot-count, not a register: the **seventy sons of Athirat** at Ugarit (KTU 1.4 VI), the **70 nations** of Genesis 10, the **70** of Jacob's house (Exod 1:5; Deut 10:22). Deut 32:8 (DSS/LXX) ties them: El Elyon divides the nations "by the number of the sons of God." Some traditions count **72**.

2. **This roster is reconstructed, not transmitted.** It triangulates: (A) **Ugaritic pantheon god-lists** (KTU 1.47, 1.118; Akkadian parallel RS 20.24), (B) **biblical "gods of the nations"** data, (C) **toponymy** — place-names that fossilize a god, and (D) **theophoric onomastics** + comparative ANE lists.

3. **Confidence is flagged** ◆ strong · ◇ moderate · ○ speculative. Treat ○ entries as mythic rhyme only.

4. **The number was never full in any one source.** Don't try to force exactly seventy. The point is the *method* and the strongest defensible names.

## How to extend this roster

To add a defensible entry, run one of the four streams:

1. **Toponym test.** Find a place-name with a divine element: `Beth-X`, `Baal-X`, `X-el`, `Migdal-X`, `Anat-`, `-shemesh`. Check whether **X** is an attested deity in Ugaritic/Mesopotamian god-lists. Hit = a fossilized son.
2. **National-god test.** Any deity the OT names as belonging to a *people* (Moab's Chemosh, Ammon's Milcom) is a portion-holder by definition under Deut 32.
3. **God-list cross-check.** Match a candidate against KTU 1.47 / 1.118 / RS 20.24 (Ugarit) or Mesopotamian An = Anum lists.
4. **Onomastic test.** Theophoric personal names (`-baal`, `-gad`, `-anat`, `-qos`) reveal which god was live in a region even when no temple survives.

Each new entry: assign a confidence flag and, if speculative, mark it ○ so the prose never leans on it as fact.

## Craft note — how the book uses this

- **The map is the buried roster.** The strongest device here is **Section C**: the divine council survives as place-names that no longer know they are god-names — Beth-Horon, Beth-Shemesh, Anathoth, Jericho. This is the **landscape-scale version of the Singing Village** keeping "Ki, Enki, the names that survived without their meanings" (`00_outline.md`). The portions of Deut 32 are still legible in the dirt.

- **A usable scene.** Aedan's cave relay or one of Eli's carrier records reads a landscape and hears the dead council in its names — a town, a hill, a spring, each a god demoted to geography. The Igigi administrators did not vanish; they sank into the map.

- **Keep the spins opposed.** Same rule as `45_divine_names.md`: each culture remembers the same council with its own theology. To Moab, Chemosh is *the* god; to Israel, he is a defeated rival son; in this canon, he is one Igigi-tier administrator of a parceled nation. Let the contradiction stand — it's evidence of the carve-up, not a problem to solve.

- **Never ancient-aliens trivia.** The payload is the wound, not the spotter's-guide. The roster matters because an orphaned species keeps the names of its withdrawn administrators long after it has forgotten what they were — and kneels to their faint echo anyway.
