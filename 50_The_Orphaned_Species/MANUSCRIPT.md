## Prologue

They come for Maren Holt on a day like any other. She has set chicken out to thaw for her supper. The dishwasher is turning through its cycle in the dark. The great catastrophes, she will learn, arrive dressed as small days.

She knows before the knock. She has always known things a half-breath before they come — the oldest trouble of her life, the reason she keeps to the end of a road with the blinds drawn. So she is already on her feet in the dark kitchen, her heart going like a struck thing, when the knock comes at last. Soft. A knuckle, not a fist. Courteous. They are always courteous. It is the first of the lies, and the kindest.

"Ms. Holt?" A woman's voice, warm, and trained to be warm. "Maren? My name is Dana — I'm with County Behavioral Health. We've had a wellness call about you; nothing to be alarmed at, I only need to lay eyes on you and we'll be on our way."

There has been no wellness call. There is no one in the world who would think to make one. Maren has no one. That was the whole purpose of the road, and the blinds.

Through the gap in the curtain she can see them plainly. A woman in a lanyard and a soft cardigan. At her shoulder, a large, becalmed man in a polo shirt. At the curb, lightless, a van without markings, its engine running. The woman holds her clipboard against her breast the way another woman might hold a child. Everything about them says the same thing: this is routine, this is for your own good, the one unforgivable act would be to make it strange.

Maren lays her hand flat against the door, as she has laid it against ten thousand things, and reads them through the grain. She has never once been wrong. The warm one in the cardigan is afraid of her, and hiding it well. The big one is bored. And beneath them both — threaded up the silent street, up through the footings of the house, into the wood beneath her palm — there is the other thing.

The cold thing. The old thing. The one she has felt all her life at the edge of every room. It is older than the road and the town and the country laid over them. It is older than the word the world has for what she is. It has hunted her kind down the whole long reach of the years — by torch, by trial, by the quiet machinery of vans and clipboards — and it has come, at last, on an ordinary day, to the end of her road.

"Maren," the warm voice says again, gently, through the wood. "Open the door, sweetheart. Don't make this hard."

She thinks of the back door. She thinks of it for exactly as long as it takes to feel the second man already standing at it, out in the dark of the yard, breathing.

When they walk her down the path to the van, her slippers are still on her feet. Across the road a porch lamp burns. At its lit window a shape is standing — a man, a glass in his hand — and Maren turns and looks at him full, the way the drowning look back at the shore. He looks back at her. After a moment he steps away from the glass, and the light goes out. The way such lights have always gone out, in every age, for every one of her kind.

No one writes her name in any book. By morning the chicken has spoiled on the counter. The dishwasher has finished and fallen still. And the house at the end of the road is only a house again, blinds drawn, exactly as it had always seemed to be.

## Chapter One

There is an hour before the city wakes that belongs to no one, and it is the only hour Eli has ever been able to call his own. He keeps it the way other men keep a faith.

The park at such an hour is a country of black and silver: frost lying like fine salt along the rubber beneath the bars, frost feathering the chain-link, his breath unspooling up through the sodium light and thinning into nothing — as though, with each breath let go, some small portion of him were being drawn off into the dark and quietly given back to the sky. From a canister buttoned in his coat he chalks his hands. He owns almost nothing, and the little he owns he keeps in an order close to reverence: the canister; a folding knife worn smooth at the hinge; a paperback swollen by a hundred rains and pressed flat again each night beneath the weight of his sleeping body; a telephone with a starred and lightless screen and no service in it, which he carries not to speak to anyone, for there is no one, but to keep some thin accounting of the hours against the long anonymity of his days.

He leaps, takes the bar, and rises.

And as he rises he counts — though the counting is in no language he was ever taught, and none whose name he has ever found. The numbers come to him as sounds with no native country in his mouth, round and throated and old as river-stones; and when the work has at last worn the watchfulness out of him they loosen and run together into something nearer a melody than a number. Three notes. Perhaps four. A small turning figure that closes upon itself — the kind of tune a man would swear on his life he knew, if only the rest of it would consent to be remembered. His mother used to hum it. It is the one thing of which he is certain, and the one thing he can never prove, for there is no longer anyone alive upon the earth to ask.

Up, and the breath goes out of him; down, and it returns. He has nothing in all the world but this — the body, the breath, the count — and out of nothing he has made it enough. A man with nothing learns, soon or late, the difference between the poor and the ruined: the poor is a weather that passes over a life, but the ruined is a way of carrying the bones, a posture of surrender, and he settled it with himself a long time ago that he would not carry himself so. He rises again.

By now the cold ought to have found his hands, and it has not. It never does. His palms lie warm against the freezing steel — warm as a low fever, warm as though somewhere beneath the skin a coal had been banked and kept, in a place to which no winter is admitted — and he gave up wondering at it years ago, the way a man gives up wondering at the shape of his own face. He does not yet know that the warmth is older than he is. He does not yet know that it has a history, and that the history is very long.

Across the grass, on a bench beyond the reach of the last lamp, a man is sitting very still in the dark, watching him — and has been watching, Eli understands, for some time.

He does not stop. A man does not stop when he is watched in the dark; he finishes the set as though he had always meant to finish it, comes down off the bar unhurried, as though the watching were nothing at all, and keeps the bag within reach of his hand.

The watcher rises from the bench. He is old — seventy, or past it — in a coat too good for the hour, his shoes soaked dark through with frost, as though he had walked out here without quite deciding to and is only now discovering where his feet have carried him. He does not come closer. He holds his distance the way a man holds back from a dog he does not know; and that, more than anything, is what keeps Eli from leaving — a stranger who understands that he is one.

"You hold the bar as though you were listening to it," the old man says.

Eli says nothing, and shoulders the bag.

"Most men fight the iron. Pull against it. You do not." The old man pauses. Something passes across his face that Eli cannot read — there, and gone, a cloud crossing a field. "Forgive me. You'll take me for one more lunatic at dawn. The city is full of us."

Eli is three steps gone when the old man begins to hum.

Three notes. Then four. A small turning figure that closes upon itself.

He stops. The frost, and the lamp, and the grey coming up behind the buildings, all of it holds still around the sound — for the sound is *his.* It is the thing he counts in; the thing with no native country in his mouth; the thing there has never, in all his life, been anyone alive to ask about. And here, now, upon a public lawn in the cold, a wet-shod old man is singing it back to him with his eyes closed — and getting it right, getting more of it right than Eli has ever once been able to hold in his own mouth at the same time.

The old man opens his eyes.

"Your mother used to hum that," he says. "Off the note. She could never quite hold the third." A breath. "No more can I, as it happens. But I know where it goes."

Eli's hands have gone cold. He turns them over without meaning to and looks down at the palms — the warmth that is always in them, that no weather has ever reached, simply gone, his hands only hands now in the raw January air. He does not know what it means, that the one constant thing in his body should choose this morning to abandon it. When he looks up, the old man is watching his hands.

"Who are you." It is not a question. He has lost, somewhere in the last minute, the power to make it one.

"Emrys. I teach at the university." A small, bitter pause, which he lets go by. "Taught. I have been looking for you a long time — longer than is reasonable. I had all but given it up, which is generally the hour at which the thing arrives." The cloud crosses his face again, the look of a man finding someone else where you are standing, and this time he does not apologise for it. "You have her hands."

He ought to walk away. Every rule he has built himself out of says *walk* — the man is strange, the morning bitter, nothing in this life that is offered freely is ever free. He has a storeroom with no name upon the door and a day to be got through, and he has got through ten thousand of them alone.

"There is coffee in my lab," Emrys says, "and a kettle that works, and no one in the place at this hour to ask either of us a single question." He is already turning to go, as though the answer were too plain to be worth the waiting for. "Come and be warm. You can decide I am mad over something hot." Then he halts, and it plainly costs him something. "And I will tell you what your mother was. And why they were afraid of her. And what it means that the cold has found your hands this morning, when in all your life it never has before — for I can see that it never has."

He walks away without once looking back to learn whether it has worked.

And after a moment, in the rising grey, Eli follows him across the wet grass.

## Chapter Two

The university at that hour is the opposite of the park.

The park was cold, but the cold was weather, and weather is honest. This is a different cold — cut stone and right angles, glass and locked brass, a cold that was designed. Eli has walked past these buildings for years the way you walk past a bank: aware that the inside is not for him, that the doors know it, that to go in is to become a question someone will eventually ask. His hands stay cold the whole way across the quad. He keeps waiting for the warmth to come back, and it doesn't, and he is surprised by how much its absence frightens him — like missing a stair that was always there in the dark.

Emrys's lab is in the basement of a building whose name Eli doesn't read. They go down a stairwell that smells of dust and old electricity, along a corridor of frosted doors gone dark — and half of them have no names, only the clean felt rectangles where the nameplates used to be, whole offices emptied and never refilled. Emrys's is the last door, the only one with light behind it. It has no name either. Just a number, and taped beneath the number, in a careful hand, a printed card: CONSCIOUSNESS & THRESHOLD STUDIES. And under that, smaller, in pen, almost an apology: *what remains of it.*

Inside is warm, and the warmth is the first kind thing the building has done.

It is one long, low room, full the way a life is full — books in stacks that have become furniture, a cot in the corner under a folded blanket, machines Eli has no names for breathing softly to themselves, a window at street height through which the morning comes gray and sideways and lies over everything like water. There is a kettle. Emrys fills it from a tap behind a curtain and sets it on, and the ordinariness of it — an old man making tea — undoes something in Eli that the singing could not.

At the far end of the room, behind a half-drawn screen, there is a chair.

It is the wrong chair for the room. Everything else here has been used soft; the chair is kept new, reclined and wired, clean lines in a nest of cable, a frame curving down close over where a head would rest. The wall behind it is panelled in dull metal, and the air near it has a held quality — the way air holds around a struck bell after the sound has gone. Eli doesn't go near it. But standing where he is, twenty feet off, the coffee Emrys has put into his hands going warm against his fingers, he feels it: the heat coming back into his palms, slow and certain, like a tide turning — and he understands, without being told, that the warmth and the chair are speaking to each other across the room, and that he is the line strung between them.

"Don't mind that," Emrys says, watching him over the rim of his cup. "It minds you quite enough for the both of you."

"You said you'd tell me what she was." Eli sets the cup down. He has not sat. Sitting is for people who are staying. "So tell me."

Emrys is quiet a moment, and it isn't the quiet of a man arranging a lie. It is the quiet of a man at the edge of a thing too large to lift cleanly.

"I knew your mother six years," he says. "We worked together. She was a researcher — a real one, better than me, though she'd have laughed to hear it. My field is an unfashionable one: life energy, the old lines the land is strung along, the current that runs under everything if you learn how to listen for it. I spent thirty years building instruments to measure what she could simply *feel*. She came to me the way you came to me — by accident, and not by accident. She could do things with her hands that the people who run this place do not believe in and are, nonetheless, very interested in. That contradiction is most of what the world is built on." He turns his cup a slow quarter. "She was frightened nearly all the time. She had reason. And she was the bravest person I have ever stood next to. After she was gone there was you — a boy somewhere in the system, slipping through the gaps in it. I have spent ten years not quite finding you. Then this autumn the warmth in your hands began to fail and catch, fail and catch — I couldn't have known that, except that I have been listening for it the way you listen for a name in a crowded room — and the city began to feel thin, as though something underneath it had started to wake. So I went out walking before dawn. For weeks. Like a fool." The cloud crosses his face again, gentler now. "And there you were on the bars, listening to the steel."

It is too much and not enough — the shape of an answer with the center cut out. Eli finds he is shaking, and can't tell whether it is grief or rage or only the warmth roaring back into his hands.

"Who's afraid of me," he says.

"Of you? No one. You're a man in a coat with cold coffee." Emrys sets his cup down. "They're afraid of what you'd be, if someone taught you what you are. And past that — more than that — they're afraid of everyone you'd teach it to after. Frightened people are easy to keep. It is the only kind of world they know how to run, and it runs on no one finding out how little they are needed."

Eli is quiet a while. Then, because the old man has handed him something true and the only true thing Eli has to hand back is the worst one: "She died when I was twelve. A car. I was in the back." He doesn't say the rest — the nine days in the hospital, the parts of him that came out not working quite right after, the gaps, the names that won't come when he reaches for them. He has never said any of it aloud to a stranger and he doesn't know why he says it now. "So whatever she was, it didn't save her."

Something moves behind Emrys's eyes and is gone before Eli can name it. The old man doesn't argue. He looks at his own hands a moment, as if checking them against a memory, and lets the silence be what it is.

"What I know is small," he says at last. "I want you to have that before you decide I'm worth the cold stairs. I know there were people who took an interest in your mother's kind. I know that when my own work came near the same ground, my funding was a line in a budget one spring and gone by the next, with no reason given — and no reason given is how you know the reason is real. I know there are players. I don't know the board." He almost smiles. "I'm a man who was shown the door very politely and has spent ten years listening at it. That is the whole of my authority. I'd rather you had it from me than learned it the hard way."

Eli should leave. The rules are very loud. But the warmth is in his hands and the room is warm and there is a cot and a kettle and an old man who knew the shape of his mother's hands, and he has been cold so long, in so many ways, that the wanting frightens him worse than the chair does.

"There's a storeroom down the hall," Emrys says — as though continuing a conversation they had been having for years. "The building's half-condemned; no one's counted the rooms in a decade. The lock's the old kind. I had a key cut, some time ago." He doesn't look at Eli as he says it, which is its own mercy. "It isn't much. It's dry, and it's warm, and the people who might mind have all had their nameplates taken off their doors. They look the other way here. It's the one thing this place is genuinely good at."

He sets a key on the table between them — small, brass, ordinary — and does not push it across.

"You don't have to decide a thing. You can finish the coffee and go back to your bars and never come down these stairs again, and I'll think no less of you, and I'll be sorry every day that's left to me."

The key lies on the table. Through the street-level window the city is coming up white, the first feet of the morning passing at the height of Eli's eyes — people going to work, to lives, none of them looking down.

He picks up the key.

## Chapter Three

The storeroom is eight feet by six and has a sink in the corner, which Eli decides is a kind of wealth. He keeps it the way he keeps everything — the blanket squared, the canister and the knife and the flattened paperback in a row along the sill, the floor swept with a brush he found in a cupboard and never mentioned. A man with a door that locks is a different animal from a man without one. He had forgotten that. It frightens him a little, how fast the animal remembers.

The days take a shape he doesn't trust. He wakes before the building does and runs — the wet stairwells when it rains, the park when it doesn't — and comes back to the kettle and the old man and the work. He eats what Emrys eats, which is little and at strange hours. He says almost nothing. Emrys lets him. It is the first place in years where his silence is taken for a kind of speech and not a defect, and he doesn't have the words for what that is worth, so he sweeps the floor instead.

There is a date he doesn't ask about. It's on a letter pinned over the bench, university letterhead curling at one corner, the language soft and final the way that language always is — *decommissioning of the lower-level laboratories* — a morning in spring, a number to call with any questions. Emrys has drawn a small neat box around the date and written nothing inside it. Eli doesn't ask. You don't ask a man about the sound his car is making when you can both already hear it. So the warmth is borrowed. He had assumed it would be. Everything he has ever been handed was on loan from someone who would, in the end, want it back.

Once, on a Thursday, crossing the market with bread under his arm, it happens on a person.

It has happened on a face before, rarely — a stranger on a bus, a child in a doorway — but never like this. A woman ahead of him in the crowd: small, quick, hood up, threading the stalls the way he threads them, like someone who learned a long time ago to be hard to follow. As she passes, the coal under his sternum does not flare so much as *lunge* — hard enough that he stops dead in the middle of the market with his heart slamming and the bread crushed to his chest. She doesn't slow. For half a second, at the edge of the stall-shadow, she half-turns, and he gets eyes, only eyes, and a stillness in them that seems to be looking at nothing and at everything at once — and then the crowd folds shut and she is gone, and the surge ebbs and leaves the cool coming after it, the way it always comes after, certain of something, certain past all reason, with not the first idea of what.

That night, in the dark of the storeroom, he turns it over the way he turns over all of them — feeling for the reason, the way you run your tongue over a chipped tooth. There isn't one. There never is. The strongest in years, and it means nothing he can find, and he files it away with the rest.

The work is slow, and mostly failure, which is a thing Eli is built for.

Emrys sits him at the bench in front of an instrument — an old hand-soldered thing, a needle behind dusty glass — and tells him to breathe, and listen, and do nothing else at all. "Don't reach for it," the old man says. "It isn't a weight. You can't lift it. You can only stop being so loud that it can't be heard." For days the needle does nothing. Then one gray afternoon, his eyes shut and his breath gone long and his open hands resting on the wood, the needle swings — once, hard — and his eyes snap open and it falls dead again.

"There," Emrys breathes, as if a star had come out. "There you are."

It is the warmth. The needle answers the heat in his hands. He can make it twitch after that, on a good day, the way you can learn to waggle your ears — but he cannot steer it, cannot hold it, cannot do the thing his mother could apparently do as easily as drawing breath. The discipline of the bars is no help to him here. The bars you conquer; you set your will against the iron and the iron gives. This you can only get out of the way of. It is the hardest thing he has ever tried to do, exactly because there is nothing to push against.

It happens on a Tuesday, with the radio on.

Emrys keeps an old radio on the bench, tuned low to a station that plays whatever it plays, and Eli has learned to stop hearing it the way he learned to stop hearing most things. Then a woman on the radio sings a word — only a word, dropped in the middle of a line he isn't listening to — and the coal under his sternum flares.

It is the old feeling. The one he has had since he was a boy, that he has never had a name for and never told a living soul. A heat that starts behind the breastbone and moves outward in a slow ring — up the throat, into the scalp — until the fine hairs on his arms lift and stand. It lasts a breath. Two. Then it ebbs, and leaves the cool behind it, the after-feeling, like the chill of a room you've only just noticed you're in.

Both his hands are flat on the bench. He doesn't remember putting them there.

When he looks up, Emrys is watching him the way he watched him on the bars — very still, very careful, unwilling to startle the thing.

"What was that," the old man says. Not *are you all right.* *What was that* — as if he already knew it was something, and only wanted its name.

And Eli, who has carried it alone for thirty years, feels the thing come up out of him before he can decide whether to let it.

"It happens on words," he says. His voice doesn't sound like his. "Since I was a kid. Not all words. There's no — there's no reason to which ones. 'Angel.' 'Courage.' A word in a song I've heard a hundred times, and the hundred-and-first it goes off like that, here—" he touches his sternum, "—and runs out to my skin, and the hair stands up, and then it's gone and I'm just sitting there. I kept a list once. Like writing it down would make it a thing instead of a symptom. I stopped." He makes himself slow down and can't. "I thought it was the broken part. The part the crash took. I never told anyone, because there's no way to say it out loud that doesn't sound like a man who belongs somewhere with locks on the outside of the doors."

Emrys is silent a long time. When he finally speaks, his voice has gone careful in a new way — the carefulness of a man lifting something he has waited a long time to hold.

"It is not the broken part," he says. "It is the part that works."

Eli doesn't answer. He isn't sure he can.

"The current I've spent my life measuring runs under everything, and most people never feel so much as a flicker of it. A few feel it. And a very few are made so that it *announces* itself — it marks things. A word, a face, a turn in a song. The body lights before the mind has the first idea why, and the meaning comes after. Years after, sometimes. You feel *that this matters* long before you can ever say *what.*" He almost smiles, and there is no happiness in it. "It is the most maddening gift in the world. To be made certain, over and over, and handed nothing to be certain *of.*"

The room is very quiet. The needle behind its glass does not move.

"Your mother kept a notebook," Emrys says. "Black cover, the spine gone soft from her hands. She wrote them down — the words, the dates, how strong each one came. She thought that if she gathered enough of them she could read the pattern off the whole, the way you read the weather off a long enough record." He looks at his own hands. "She never finished it. But she was certain there was a shape in it. She was certain it was trying to *say* something."

It lands in Eli the way the surges land — first in the body, the meaning trailing after. *She kept a list.* He kept a list. He is not broken, and he is not the only one, and he has not, it turns out, been entirely alone in it: somewhere ahead of him in the dark a woman with his hands had been keeping the same impossible record, and she thought it *meant* something.

"Where is it," he says. "The notebook."

The old man's face does the thing again — the cloud, the something moving under the surface — and this time Eli watches it all the way through and still cannot read it.

"Gone," Emrys says. "With a great deal else, the night I lost her. I've tried. You would be surprised what a defunded old man cannot get returned to him." He gathers the cups, though they are empty, because his hands need something to do. "I'm sorry. I'd give a great deal to put it in your hands. There's a part of me that believes it's the only thing left in the world that is truly hers."

Eli sits with that. Above them the building is filling with the day — feet, doors, the institution waking around the dark room like a tide coming in over a stone.

He has spent his whole life wanting nothing he couldn't carry. It was the only way to stay light enough to keep moving, to keep surviving, to keep from being the kind of man the world could wound by taking something from him. And now, in a condemned basement, across a bench from an old man who knew the shape of his mother's hands, he feels it begin in him anyway — the dangerous thing, the thing he has refused himself for ten years.

He wants. He wants to know what she knew. He wants the black notebook with the ruined spine, and the pattern in it, and what the pattern was trying to say. He wants — God help him, he can feel the size of it — not to be the last one left who carries this.

The coal under his sternum is warm again. It has been warm, he understands, since the moment he picked up the key.

## Chapter Four

The cold comes into his hands an hour before the man does.

Eli has learned, these weeks, to read the warmth — when it floods, when it ebbs, the way a sailor learns a sky. This is something new. This is the warmth *fleeing*, drawing down out of his fingers and back toward the coal under his sternum as though taking shelter, and by the time the knock comes at the lab door — soft, a knuckle, polite — his hands are as cold as they were that first morning on the bars, and he understands that his body has been trying to tell him something for an hour and he had no language for it yet.

Emrys goes very still at the sound. It isn't the careful, not-wanting-to-startle stillness of the bench. It is the stillness of an animal that has heard one particular footstep before.

"Sit," he says quietly, not turning. "At the bench. Look at the instrument. Whatever happens, you are a research subject and nothing else. You don't speak unless he speaks to you, and when he does you are slow, and you are dull, and you are not worth a second thought. Can you do that."

"I've done it my whole life," Eli says.

"Good." A breath. "Then you'll be convincing."

The man Emrys lets in is tall and unhurried, dressed the way money dresses when it would rather not be noticed — a gray that costs more than color. Perhaps sixty, with a face built for committees, pleasant and symmetrical and entirely closed, and when he smiles, which he does at once, the smile arrives a half-second ahead of any warmth that might have justified it, like a light switched on in a room with no one in it.

"Gwyn," he says — and Eli files it, the old man's first name, a thing he never thought to wonder about — "forgive the hour. I was in the building."

"No one is ever in this building, Marcus." Emrys does not offer his hand. "That was rather the point of giving it to me."

"I came myself because I didn't want you hearing it from a letter." The man stands in the middle of the room and lets his eyes travel it — the books, the cot, the soft machines, the screen at the far end and what stands behind it — and his face does not change at any of it, which is its own kind of statement. "The date's moved up. They're surveying the lower floors for the new imaging suite. Your old funding line, more or less; there's a joke in that somewhere. They want it cleared by the end of the month." A small, regretful tilt of the head. "I argued for summer. I want you to know I argued."

"I'm sure you were eloquent."

"Gwyn." His voice goes gentle, and the gentleness is the worst thing yet to enter the room. "Thirty years. No one publishes. No one replicates. The grant officers stopped returning the calls long before I had to make any. I didn't do this to you. I have spent capital I won't get back making it slower and kinder than it would otherwise have been. There's an emeritus office on the third floor with a window in it. Let me give you the window. Let the rest go."

For a moment Emrys says nothing, and Eli — slow, dull, watching the dead needle behind its glass — feels the old man *want* it. The window. The rest. Feels how tired he is, how long he has been tired.

"You always did know precisely how much to offer," Emrys says at last. "Never enough to keep. Just enough to make refusing it feel like the madness." His smile is thin. "Thank you, Marcus. No."

It is then that Crane looks at Eli.

He has been in the room four minutes and not once looked at him, and Eli understands, too late, that this was not because he hadn't noticed him. It was the opposite. He had been saving him.

"And who's this."

"A subject," Emrys says, easy, bored. "Volunteer. He sits, I take readings, I give him his bus fare. The insurers would faint, so we won't trouble them."

"Mm." Crane crosses the room. He does it slowly, and Eli watches the cold in his own hands deepen with every step the man takes — the warmth fled all the way to the coal now, and the coal itself gone small and still, hiding. Crane stops in front of him and puts out his hand, and there is nothing to do that a dull man would do but take it.

The hand is cold. Colder than the room, colder than a living hand has any business being — a cold that has been kept a long time. And the instant their palms meet, the coal under Eli's sternum does the thing it does. It flares. Except it is wrong, it is backwards: not the warm outward ring of the surges but a single hard pull *inward*, a hook set in the center of him, as though something in this gray cold man had recognized something in Eli and reached for it across a great and lightless distance.

It lasts less than a second. Eli keeps his face dull. He has kept his face dull through worse.

But Crane has gone very still.

For one moment the committee-face is simply gone, and the thing beneath it looks at Eli with an expression he has no name for — not anger, not even surprise. Something nearer to grief. Something nearer to *recognition*, and to the swift, practiced violence with which the man shuts it away again, the way you would slam a door on a room you have promised yourself you will never enter.

"Bus fare," Crane says softly, and lets the hand go. "How kind of you, Gwyn." He steps back, and the face reassembles, seamless and pleasant. But his eyes rest on Eli a half-second too long, and Eli knows — the way he knows a word before he can read it — that he has just been written down somewhere, in a careful hand, on a list.

When the door has closed behind him, Emrys stands looking at it a long while.

"He felt you," he says. Not a question.

"I didn't do anything. I kept it down—"

"You kept your *face* down. You can't keep the other thing down — you don't have the reins yet, that's the whole of the trouble." Emrys turns, and he looks older than he did an hour ago, older and suddenly, frighteningly, decided. "He felt you, and he is not a man who feels anything by accident anymore. Which means he knew it for what it was. Which means we have only as long as it takes him to decide what to do about it. Days. Perhaps fewer."

He crosses to the screen and draws it back, and there is the chair — clean and waiting in its nest of cable, the dull metal of the wall behind it, the held silence in the air.

"I meant to do this slowly," he says. "Over months. The way she would have wanted. The way you deserve — to come to it ready, and rested, and knowing what it is." He lays one hand on the headframe, almost tenderly. "We don't have months. We have what he's left us. The careful road and the safe road were always the same road, and he just took it, the way he takes everything — politely, and without leaving a mark."

The cold has not gone out of Eli's hands. But across the room the chair waits, and for the first time since the park he feels the warmth try to return — not to his hands, but *toward the chair*, reaching for it the way iron reaches for a magnet, the way a thing reaches for the other half of itself.

"What is it," Eli says. "The chair."

"It's the threshold. It's the door your mother spent her life trying to describe to me." Emrys looks at him, and there is fear in his face now, real fear, the first Eli has seen there. "And it's the only way I know to give you in days what should have taken you years. It nearly killed the last person who sat in it. I need you to know that before you decide. I need you to know I would never ask it — if he had left us any other road at all."

## Chapter Five

He sits in the chair because the alternative is to be taken, and he has been taken before — by smaller men than Crane, into smaller rooms — and he knows what is on the far side of being taken. At least this, he chooses.

"Tell me what it does," he says. He is already in the chair. The leather is cold, and the frame curves down over his head like the inside of a bell.

"It takes you to the edge." Emrys works as he talks, fitting soft contacts to Eli's temples, his chest, the thin skin inside his wrists, and his hands do not quite manage to stop shaking. "There is a threshold every living thing crosses once, at the very end, and on the far side of it the current — the field, whatever you want to call it; your mother had a hundred names — runs without a body in the way. She could stand at that threshold and look across it whenever she pleased. It was as natural to her as you holding your breath. Most people couldn't find it in a lifetime of trying. This is thirty years of my life spent building a door for the ones who can't find their own. It brings you to the edge and holds you there. Long enough to see."

"And the danger."

He is quiet a moment. "The edge is the edge. The body, brought to it, wants to keep going over. The whole art is in the holding — taking you close enough that the locks in you open, and drawing you back before the rest of you follows. Done slowly, over months, it is merely terrifying." He meets Eli's eyes. "Done in an afternoon, on a man never taught to find his own way home, it is something else. Past a certain point I cannot reach you. You would not die, exactly. You would simply not come back. The thing that woke in this chair would be everyone you have ever been, and no one I could call Eli."

"That's dissolution."

"That's dissolution."

"Did she sit in it." Eli already knows the answer. "My mother."

"Once." The old man's hands go still. "To prove to me it could be survived. I begged her not to. She said someone had to know what they were asking of people, before they asked it." He swallows. "It very nearly didn't give her back. I swore to her, after, that I would never put another living soul in this chair." He almost smiles, and it is a terrible thing to see. "And here I am. She'd be so angry with me. God, she would be furious."

Eli lies back. The frame closes its cold mouth around his head. Across the room the instruments begin to wake — a low building hum he feels in his teeth, in the fillings of his teeth, in the old crack the crash left in his skull.

"If I don't come back," he says. "The notebook. What she knew. If you ever find it—"

"You'll read it yourself."

"If I don't."

"Then I'll read it aloud, in this room, to no one, every year until I die. Now. Breathe — long and slow, you know how. You taught yourself on the bars; you only didn't know what you were teaching." His voice gentles, going far away. "Breathe. And when the floor drops out from under you — and it will — don't grab for the edge. There is no edge. There never was. You hold *yourself*, Eli. You're the only thing you've ever managed to hold. Hold that."

The hum becomes a pressure becomes a light behind his eyes, and the floor drops out from under him.

And then there is no floor, and there is no Eli, and there is a roaring, and the roaring is voices, and the voices are all his.

He is a man kneeling in cold dust with a chisel in his fist and a fox taking shape under it and a stillness opening behind his eyes wider than the sky. He is a girl of nineteen with her palms held above a fevered child, reading the heat off the small body like a script. He is a boy in a black pit with grain pouring over him and his mother's hand on his face saying *go, go somewhere else in your mind, go now.* He is old, so old, his hands deep in wet bog-moss, speaking to something green and slow and enormous that speaks back. He is a young woman holding one note in her throat until the light bends at the edge of the world. He is none of them and all of them, and the thing that is meant to stand in the middle holding them is coming apart like wet paper in a current.

This is the thing they never tell you about being everyone. It does not feel like more. It feels like *less.* It feels like a drop of ink remembering, too late, that it was once a whole sea, and dispersing back into the water almost gratefully — and there is a sweetness in it, a terrible sweetness, the sweetness of setting down a weight you have carried so long you forgot it was yours to set down. And Eli feels himself begin, with something close to relief, to let go.

Far away, in a room with a kettle and a cot, a voice is calling a name. The name means nothing. It is a small dry sound, one word in ten thousand years of words, and there is no reason on earth to swim back toward one small dry sound when the whole warm sea is asking him, so kindly, to stay.

He is going to stay.

He is going.

And then, in the dispersing, there is a shape.

It is not a voice, and it is not the old man, and it is not anything he reaches for; it simply *is,* the way the surges simply are — it lands in the body before the mind, a knowing with no words yet attached. Three rings. Three circles, one inside the next, drawn in light on the dark, and something is moving through them: a warmth, the warmth, *his* warmth, beginning at the center and moving outward, ring to ring to ring — and where it passes the dark goes clear, and where it fails to pass the dark goes foul.

The innermost ring is the size of a body. It is the size of *his* body. And in the roaring sea of everyone he has ever been, it is the one small thing that is only, exactly, here: the cold leather beneath him, the crack in his skull, the breath going out and the breath coming back, the body on the bars, the body that had nothing and made itself enough. The drop of ink, dispersing, finds in the dark the shape of the cup it fell from.

The words come after, the way they always come after — late, and small, and certain. *What is real moves outward. What is false only leaks.*

He does not understand it. He does not have to. He has spent his whole life being handed certainties with nothing to be certain of, and here, at last, is the thing the certainty was *for.* He is the warmth. He begins at the center. He holds the innermost ring, the one that is only him, and he does not grab for the edge, because there is no edge — there is only the center, and from the center he begins, with everything he has, to move back outward, toward one small dry sound that is, he remembers now, his name.

*Eli.*

He takes it like a handhold. And he climbs.

He comes back the way a diver comes up too fast — wrong, and in pain, and gasping.

The first thing is the ceiling, water-stained, real. The second is Emrys's face above him, gray as ash and streaming — the old man is weeping openly, with no attempt to hide it — his lips moving, the sound arriving from very far off and coming slowly near until it is a word and the word is *Eli, Eli, there you are, oh thank god, there you are.*

He is in the chair. He is in his body, and his body hurts everywhere, the deep ache of muscles that have been screaming. His own face is wet. The contacts are off and the frame is up and he remembers neither. The hum is gone. The room is only a room again, low and warm — the kettle, the cot, the gray window full of an ordinary afternoon.

"How long," he says. His voice is wreckage.

"Four minutes." Emrys laughs, a cracked and terrible sound. "Four minutes, and I have aged a hundred years. Your heart stopped. Twice. I had my hands on your chest, I was three seconds from—" He stops, and wipes his face with the heel of his hand, furious at himself. "You came back. You found the way back. I have never seen anyone find the way back — not the first time, not without years of it—" He looks at Eli with something like terror and something like awe. "What did you find out there. How did you come back."

Eli looks at his hands. They are warm. They are warm all the way through, warmer than the bars, warmer than the coal, a warmth that does not ebb when he watches it — and when he lifts them into the gray light the air shivers faintly around his fingers, the way air shivers over a hot road in summer.

He is not the same. He knows it the way you know you have crossed a border in the night: nothing you can point to, everything changed. The roaring is still there, far down, held now behind a thin and trembling wall — all of them, all the ones he was, waiting. And he understands, with the cold clarity of the surges, two things at once.

The first is that the wall will not hold for long; that learning to hold it — to sort the roaring sea into one voice, one life, one Eli, before it drowns him for good — is the only work that will keep him alive now.

The second is that somewhere above them, in a clean office in a cold building, a gray man who shook his hand this morning is already deciding what to do about him. And that the next time a soft knock comes at a door — polite, a knuckle and not a fist — it will be his door, and his name on the clipboard, and no one on the street will look up.

Across the room, behind its glass, the dead needle stands at the very top of its arc, hard against the pin, and does not fall.

# PART TWO

## Chapter Six

They come on the third night.

Eli has barely slept since the chair. Every time he closes his eyes the wall thins and the roaring leans against it — all the ones he was, testing — and he has learned in three days to keep one hand always on the inner ring, the small warm circle that is only him, the way you keep a hand on a door in a high wind. Emrys has not slept either. The old man moves through the dark lab gathering things into a canvas bag — papers, a hard drive, the black-cased instrument with the needle — with the terrible calm of a man who decided something days ago and is only now letting his hands carry it out.

"When it's light," he is saying, "we go. There's a friend, north, who owes me a great deal and asks no questions. It isn't safe. It's only safer. We'll have an hour after dawn before anyone thinks to—"

The knock comes at twenty past three.

It is soft. A knuckle, not a fist. Polite.

Emrys does not move toward the door. He moves, fast for an old man, to the bench, and his hand closes hard on Eli's arm. "The service stair," he breathes. "Behind the boiler. It runs up to the loading dock. Go now, go quiet, don't run until the street—"

"Both of us. You're coming—"

"Eli."

And then there is a third voice in the dark, low and very close, from the direction of the old coal door behind the boiler that Eli has never once seen standing open. "He's not coming. Move."

She has come up out of the black like she was poured there. In the bad light Eli gets almost nothing of her — eyes, the dull gleam of a blade held flat along her forearm, and a certainty so absolute he has no time to question it.

"I'm not leaving him," Eli says.

"You're not *saving* him." Flat, fast, a whisper with an edge on it. "Six in the stairwell, two on the dock. The only reason you and I are breathing is they don't know I'm here, and that lasts about nine more seconds. Come with me and live, or stay and hold his hand while they take you both — which helps him exactly nothing. Choose."

At the front of the lab the lock turns. Not forced. *Turned* — they have a key, of course they have a key, they have everything — and Emrys puts both hands flat on Eli's chest and shoves him backward into the dark with a strength Eli would never have guessed was in him.

"She's right. Go." His face — the last of him Eli will see — is not afraid. It is lit, almost. Fierce, and strangely free: the face of a man who has waited ten years to be of some use and has just been handed the bill and is *glad* to pay it. "Find the notebook. Find what your mother knew. And learn to hold yourself — you hear me — *hold yourself—*"

The door is opening. A cardigan, a lanyard, a warm trained voice already coming through it: *Gwyn. There you are. We did knock.*

Then Wren's hand is a manacle on his wrist and she hauls him into the coal-dark, and the last of Emrys is his voice, rising, deliberately loud, throwing itself the other way to pull them after it: "*Marcus.* You came yourself. I'm flattered—"

And the dark takes the rest.

They go up. Not the way he came in — a forgotten way, a brick shaft with iron rungs slick with a hundred years of damp, and she goes up it ahead of him fast and silent and he follows because there is nothing else left to do, his hands finding rungs his eyes can't see, the roaring leaning hard on the wall inside him now, the panic feeding it. Far below, a sound begins that he will spend a long time refusing to name.

At the top of the shaft she stops him — a hand flat on his sternum, *wait* — and they stand jammed into a doorway in the dark while boots cross somewhere ahead and recede. And it is then, with her hand on his chest exactly over the place the coal lives, that it happens.

Not the cold hook of the gray man. The opposite of it. The warmth under her palm does not flee — it *rises,* goes toward her hand like a tide coming in, and the coal flares outward, the right way, the surge way, up his throat and into his scalp and lifting the hair on his arms, and for one impossible second — in the dark of a condemned building with men coming to kill them both — Eli is filled to the brim with the most absurd and total certainty he has ever felt. A certainty with nothing to be certain of. The oldest kind.

*There you are. I have been looking for you. I have been looking for you for so long.*

He doesn't say it. He would sooner die, and may shortly get the chance.

But her hand on his chest has gone very still. And in the dark he knows — the way he is learning to know things — that she felt it too, the same impossible thing from the other side, and that it frightens her more than the men in the stairwell, and that she is already, even now, deciding it never happened.

"Move," she says. But her voice has changed, just slightly, and she takes her hand back like it cost her something.

They come out through a roof, a fire escape, a gap in a fence, into an alley four streets off. The city at this hour is the city of his old life — wet, sodium-lit, indifferent, the hour that belongs to no one — except that he will never get to belong to no one again, and he understands that now. The breach burned it off him. He is loud now. He will be loud the rest of his life. They found him because he rang like a bell; they will keep finding him.

In the mouth of the alley she turns and looks at him properly, in the light, for the first time.

And he knows her. The certainty lands ahead of the memory, the way it always does — and then the memory comes: the market, weeks back, the bread crushed to his chest, the surge that *lunged.* The hooded woman in the crowd who was hard to follow. It was her. Of course it was her.

What's in her face is not warmth. It's calculation, and exhaustion, and a wariness so deep it has gone all the way around into a kind of grief.

"What did you *do,*" she says. "Three nights ago. Something lit the grid for fifty miles. Half of us felt it in our teeth. I came to see what died." Her eyes drop to his hands, the air still moving faintly over them, and her jaw sets. "Instead I find a brand-new one, untrained, sitting in the open in a building they've watched for years, with an old man who should have known better. You rang the dinner bell. And then you sat down at the table."

"They took him." It is the only thing in him. "Because of me."

"Yes."

"I'm going to get him back."

Something crosses her face that is worse than pity — the look of a person watching someone believe a thing she used to believe. "No. You're going to learn to disappear, in the next four hours, or you'll be in a van by dawn — and the old man, and whatever else you think you're going to save, none of it happens if you're gone. Dead men rescue no one."

"Then teach me." It comes out raw. "To disappear. To hold this. You came to see what died — I'm what didn't. Teach me, and I'll be worth the trouble. More than the trouble."

She stares at him. By every rule she has — and Eli can see that she lives by rules, hard ones, the way he does; one more thing they share — she should walk away from him now. He is a flare in a dark wood, the loudest, most findable thing on the street, and tying herself to him is a death sentence with extra steps. He can see her knowing it.

And she doesn't walk away.

He watches her not walk away, and watches it cost her, and understands that whatever moved between them in the stairwell is moving still, under the wariness, under the rules — an undertow she is pretending not to feel, because the alternative is to admit she crossed a city toward a ringing bell for a reason she cannot name.

"If you slow me down, I leave you," she says at last. "If you go loud, I leave you. If I tell you to do a thing, you do it before you've finished deciding whether you will. And you don't"— something catches, and she masters it —"you don't look at me like that. Like you know me. You don't know me."

"What do I call you."

She's quiet a moment, as though even this is a thing she has to decide whether she can afford to spend.

"Wren."

"Eli."

"I know what you are," she says. "You're the only thing anyone's talking about." And she turns and goes, fast, into the dark between the streetlights — and after a moment the loudest thing in the city gathers itself and follows the one person who came toward the noise instead of away from it.

## Chapter Seven

They go to ground in the way Wren has made an art of — buses paid in coins, an hour of stolen sleep in the storeroom of a night chemist, a car lifted from a long-stay lot with a coat-hanger and a held breath — and by the second night they are out of the city and into the old country, the low green dark of fields and dry-stone walls, and Eli is coming apart.

He had believed, in the chair, that the worst of it was the going-under. He understands now that the worst is the after. The wall that Emrys's voice and the three rings of light had let him raise is not a wall at all but a held breath, and no man holds his breath forever. The dead press against it. They rise in him at the wrong moments — he is passing Wren a stolen sandwich and his hands are abruptly a woman's hands, smaller, certain, closing the eyes of a child that has died; he is watching the road unspool in the headlights and the road becomes a river and he is poling a reed boat down it beneath a sky hung with the wrong stars — and each time he claws his way back to the small warm circle that is only himself, and each time it is harder, and the circle is smaller, and he is more tired.

Wren watches him the way you watch a fire you are no longer sure you can keep. She knows how to run; she does not know what this is. Twice she has taken the wheel out of his hands. Once, in the grey before dawn, she woke to find him gone from the car and walking out across a black field toward nothing she could see, his bare feet in the frost and his face turned up to the failing stars; and when she caught him and turned him round, his eyes did not know her for a long moment, and the thing that looked out of them was older than he was, and very far away. When at last he came back into his own face he wept — which frightened her worse than all the rest of it, because she had not understood, until then, how young he was.

It is he who turns them off the road. He cannot say why. There is a pull in his hands, in the banked warmth of him — the same blind certainty as the chair across the lab, iron leaning toward a magnet, a thing reaching after the other half of itself — and it draws him down a farm track and over a stile and up a long dark field in the last of the night, Wren cursing low behind him, until they come over the rise, and he stops, because the field is not empty.

They stand in a ring against the paling sky: stones, old stones, hunched and shawled in lichen, no two of them alike, leaning to one another like a council of the very old who have argued so long they have forgotten the quarrel and kept only the company. Seventy of them, or more; he will learn afterward that no one has ever twice counted them to the same number, and that the counting is itself a part of what they are. The King's Men, the local people call them, when they call them anything: a king and his army turned to stone upon this hill by a witch, and bound to stand until the spell breaks and the world ends.

Eli does not know the story, and does not need it. He knows only that the stones are awake, and that they have been waiting — not for a man, not for him in particular, but the way a struck bell waits within its own metal for the next striking. And as he steps inside the ring the warmth comes up out of him in a flood, not into his hands now but outward, the whole of him, toward the stones and into the stones and down through the stones into the cold turned earth and the dark beneath it; and the stones answer.

"Eli." Wren's voice has gone thin as wire. "Eli — whatever this is, don't. They'll feel it. This is the loudest place for fifty miles and you are about to be the loudest thing in it — they'll feel it from the *city,* do you hear me—"

He hears her. He cannot help her. His hand is already flat against the nearest stone — and it is warm, warm as his own palm, warm as a living flank — and the door that has strained behind his eyes since the chair simply, at last, swings open.

He hears, very far away, Wren cry out, and feels her hands close on his shoulders, and the morning tilt as his knees let go beneath him; and then the field and the stones and the girl and the whole thin bright skin of the present age pour away from him like water down a drain, and he goes down, and down, and down — past the road and the city, past the war, past his mother and the crash and the boy on the bars; down past the legions and the druids and the long retreat into the west; down past the kings and the first walled cities and the drowned and forgotten years; down through eleven and a half thousand turnings of the world — and the plain speech of the present age, the only register in which he has ever lived, gives way beneath him to something older and slower and without hurry or mercy, and he comes to rest, and opens eyes that are not his own, upon the morning of the world.

In that age the world is young, and the people are few, and they have not yet learned to break the earth and sow it, nor to pen the wild things, nor to set one man above another and name it the will of heaven. They are hunters still, and gatherers of the wild grain; they go in small bands beneath a wide sky. But once in the turning of the year, when the grain stands gold upon the hills, the bands come in from all the country around to the high place above the plain, and there, together, they raise the stones.

He is among them. He is one of them. The name in his own mouth is Aru.

He is young, and his hands are good, and that is why they have set him to the work. All his life he has watched the elders raise the great pillars — taller than three men standing, shaped after the body of someone who is not quite a man, with arms folded in relief upon the belly and a belt of carved stone about the waist — and stand them in their rings upon the hill; and all his life he has wondered who they are, these tall still ones, and no one will say, and perhaps no one any longer knows, and to ask the question too loudly is not done. His own work is the lesser stone. Into the pillars, and the slabs set between them, he cuts the living things: the running fox, the vulture with its wings flung wide as a man's reach, the great horned aurochs, the crane, the snake, the scorpion. Why these and not others he does not know either. He knows only that the fox is his — that his foxes are finer than any other carver's, and that when he has cut one well the old people lay their hands upon it and fall silent.

There is a woman who carries water to the work, old past the counting of her years, with a face like the bark of an oak, and her name is Selé. She has watched him a long while. One day, when his chisel slips and ruins a line and he curses and throws it down, she sets the water-skin in the dust at his side and says: "You cut as though the stone were deaf. It is not deaf. Be still, and let it learn whose hand is upon it, before you cut again."

He takes her for mad, as the very old are sometimes mad. But she does not go away, and there is nothing to be done under her watching but the thing she asks; and so he sits before the half-cut fox with the chisel idle in his lap and does what she says, which is nothing, which is only to be still — to let the breath go out, and come, and go — and to wait.

For a long time nothing comes. The hill is loud with the labour: the knock of stone on stone, the chant of the men hauling a pillar up the ramp, the wind off the plain. His thoughts run and run — the ruined line, his hunger, a girl in the camp below, the ruined line again — and he watches them run, because there is nothing else to do; and somewhere in the watching, without his ever deciding it, something quietly steps back.

It is the smallest thing in all the world, and the largest. One moment he is his thoughts, tangled in them and carried along; and the next he is the one who watches the thoughts, standing a little behind them, in a stillness that has lain there the whole time beneath the noise the way the sky lies the whole time behind the weather. The thoughts go on. But he is no longer only the thoughts. He is also the quiet that holds them — and the quiet has no need of the carver, no need of the fox or the ruined line or the girl or even the breath; it is simply there, watching, entire, and it was there before he was born and will be there after he is gone; and for one endless moment upon the morning of the world Aru the carver rests in the thing behind his own eyes and is afraid of nothing under heaven, because there is nothing left in him that can be touched.

When he comes back — and he does come back, the hill loud again about him, his legs gone to sleep beneath him — he takes up the chisel, and into the eye of the fox he is carving he cuts a small thing that no one has asked of him and no rite requires: a spiral, turning inward, a coil that winds down and down to a still point at its centre. It is the only language he has for what has happened to him. He does not understand it. He knows only that it is true.

A child is watching him: a small one, a sweeper of stone-dust, with grave eyes; and the child alone, of all the people upon the hill, sees the spiral go into the fox's eye, and looks from it to Aru's face and back again, and says nothing, and understands everything. Her name is Sib. She will carry the memory of it the whole of her life. She will cut it, an old woman, into a stone of her own; and the one who watches *her* will carry it on; and so it will go down the long chain of the keepers, hand to hand and eye to eye, eleven thousand years, until it surfaces at last in the dream of a boy upon a pull-up bar who counts in a language no one ever taught him. But Aru cannot know this, and it is better that he does not. He knows only that the child saw — and that he is, for the first time in his life, not alone in the thing he cannot name.

He lives the rest of it, and it is a life. He carves through that season and the next, and others after. He takes the girl from the camp to wife, and loses her one hard winter to a child that will not be born. He grows the stillness in himself the way a man grows anything, by returning to it; and it carries him through the grief as it carried him through the ruined line, for the quiet behind his eyes is touched by neither. He becomes, in the long turning of the years, an old man of whom the young carvers are a little afraid — who sits too still, who teaches one or two of them, the ones with the grave eyes, to be still and let the stone learn the hand. And one autumn he does not climb the hill, and so they carry him up it instead, and lay him in the earth at the foot of a pillar he cut when he was young, with a fox upon it, and a small spiral turning in the fox's eye. And the wild grain stands gold upon the hills, and the bands come together as they have always come, and the stones go up into the wide sky, and the world is still young.

And Eli, who has been all of this — who has been born, and married, and widowed, and grown old, and died upon the morning of the world — is torn up out of it the way a hook tears a fish from deep water: fast, and backward, and against the whole black weight of the deep. The eleven thousand years come back the other way in a single roaring instant, the dead years flooding past him in reverse — the graves and the kings and the long retreat, and Rome, and his mother, and the crash, and the bars — and he breaks the surface of his own age with a sound he does not know for his own voice. The first thing he knows is grass, cold and wet against his cheek. The second thing is Wren.

She is on her knees over him within the ring of stones, both fists knotted in his collar, her face a single white question, saying his name — not gently, the way Emrys said it, but the way you say a name to haul someone back off a ledge, hard, and over, and over. Behind her the sky has gone the grey of ash, and the stones stand round them in the dawn precisely as they stood, patient, having given him an entire life and asked nothing for it but the using.

He is weeping and cannot stop, and it is not for himself. It is for Aru, dead these eleven thousand years, whom he loved, whom he *was,* whose whole life he has lived and lost — he will learn — in the space of four minutes by Wren's reckoning. It is for the girl in the camp, and the child that would not come, and the fox in the stone. It is the particular grief of the descents, which he will come to know well and never learn to bear: to live a life entire, and love its people with the whole of a heart, and wake to find them dust eleven thousand years, the love still hot in you and nowhere upon the earth to set it down.

But beneath the grief — beneath it, holding it, the way the sky holds the weather — there is the stillness. He has carried it up with him. It is his now: the watcher behind the thought, the quiet with no need of the carver; and when the dead rise in him now, the woman's hands, the reed boat, the wrong stars, he no longer claws at the small warm circle and loses ground. He simply steps back, into the still place Selé gave to a boy on the morning of the world, and lets the dead come, and lets them go, and is not moved. The wall is no longer a held breath. It is a sky.

"Eli." Wren's voice is shaking, and her eyes are no longer on him but on the lightening field below the hill, the farm track, the grey ribbon of road beyond it. "Eli, get up. Get up *now.* You did it — you did the loud thing — and I think—" She swallows. Down on the far road, slowing, turning in at the farm track, there is a van; and behind it another; no markings on either, and lightless still, though the day has come. "I think they heard you in the city."

He gets up. The stillness gets up with him. And the stones, the old patient council of the King's Men, stand round the two of them in the dawn and say nothing — having watched, upon this hill, the coming and the going of every kind of doom, for eleven thousand years.

## The Medicine

The road to Pai climbs out of the lowland heat in seven hundred and sixty-two curves. Eli has the number from a sticker on a guesthouse wall, passed traveller to traveller the way such numbers are passed, like charms against a thing not yet named; and he takes the curves one at a time on a borrowed motorbike with a slipping clutch, up through the green dark of the hills, the jungle leaning in close and dripping, the switchbacks folding back upon themselves so steeply that twice he meets his own dust still hanging in the air above the curve below.

He is climbing toward something he could not name if you asked him. That has become the shape of the whole journey: a pull in the hands, in the banked warmth of him, drawing him up out of one country and into the next — the way it drew him off the road into the field of stones at home, the way it has drawn him since, east, and east, always east and older, as though the world grew more truly itself the further back along its own road he travelled.

He does not reach the top.

It is a curve like all the others, but the rain has washed gravel loose across it, and the front wheel goes from under him without warning and without mercy, and the bike and the man part company, and the world becomes for one bright spinning instant a thing of sky and red dust and green — and then the ground, and the long tearing graze of it, and stillness, and the engine ticking somewhere off in the ferns, and his own blood, very red and very ordinary, running down into the dust of a mountain on the far side of the earth from anywhere he has ever belonged.

He does not know how long he lies there. When he comes back to himself there are men around him — lean brown men in cut leather, riders, a whole ragged line of them halted along the curve with their machines idling — and they are not unkind. They gather him up the way you gather a thing that is broken but not yet dead. One of them says something Eli cannot understand and laughs, not cruelly. They set him on the back of the largest machine, wedged between a man's broad back and a stranger's steadying arm, and they carry him — not down the mountain to any hospital, but up it, the last curves, into Pai.

The place they bring him to is a bar at the edge of the town where the jungle begins again: a long low room open to the night on one side, strung with bare bulbs and the blue smoke of clove cigarettes, full of the particular hush of people who have travelled a great way in order to be no one for a while. They sit him down. A girl cleans the gravel from his arm with a cloth and a brown bottle and a gentleness that undoes him a little. Someone puts a drink in his hand that he does not drink. And in the corner, on a low stool, beneath the one bulb that does not flicker, there is an old man, playing.

He is old past the guessing of it, the old man — brown and folded as a fallen leaf — and he is playing some small thing of gourd and string, and singing over it, low, in a language that is not Thai and is not any tongue Eli has ever heard: a song with no edges to it, that seems to rise up out of the floor and the hills and the dark rather than out of any human throat. And the coal beneath Eli's sternum, which has lain quiet since the crash, stirs, and turns toward the song the way a green thing turns toward a window.

The old man stops. He looks at Eli across the smoke for a long moment — the way Emrys looked at him on the bars, the way the woman in the cardigan looked at Maren Holt through a curtain: the look of one who is reading, and not merely seeing — and then he says something to the riders, and the riders go still, and one of them brings up from the back a bottle of something dark.

"It is not a thing to take lightly," the old man tells him — in English now, slow and broken, and without any warmth at all. He does not smile. "I will not tell you it is safe. It is not safe. Some who drink it open like a door. Some break like a cup. I have buried two." He lets the words lie there in the smoke. "I do not know which you are. No one knows, before."

He studies Eli a while longer — that reading look, the look of a man weighing a thing on a scale only he can see. "But the warmth has marked you," he says at last, more quietly. "I have met a few, in a long life, who carry it. You are one. Whatever you are running from is already inside you. The cup does not put it there. The cup only opens the door."

He pours the dark thing into a cup no larger than a thumb. He does not yet hold it out.

"There is a tree," he says, "and out of the tree, a vine; and the vine is bitter. And the priests, the fathers, the men with the keys — they say to your people, and have said it a long time: *there is a tree, and you shall not eat of the fruit of it; for in the day that you eat thereof, you shall surely die.*" Something moves in the folded old face, and it is not kindness. "Maybe they lie. Maybe they only fear what you would become. Or maybe they tell the truth — maybe the fruit is death, and they forbid it to spare you. I have never known which. I carry it, and I pour it, and I watch to see which way a man falls." Now he holds out the cup. "You choose."

By every rule Eli has ever lived by, he should set the cup down and walk out into the night.

But the rules are quiet in him tonight. The crash has knocked them loose — his skull is ringing, the room tilts when he turns his head too fast, the blood has dried stiff and dark along his arm — and a man thrown from a mountain three hours ago does not frighten easily at a cupful of bitter water. He has been close to death already tonight. He felt it go past him on the curve, near as a held breath. Having felt it pass, the fruit of some tree frightens him less than it ought to. And the riders are watching him — the men who peeled him off the road and did not have to — and there is no malice in their faces, only a grave, patient attention, as though they too would like to see which way he falls. He trusts them. He could not say why. He trusts the whole strange, warm, dangerous night, the way the stunned and the half-dead will trust.

He lifts the cup. It is the most bitter thing that has ever been in his mouth — bitter as the root of the world — and he drinks it down. The old man takes up the gourd and the string again, and begins, very quietly, to sing.

For a long while nothing comes, and he thinks, dully, that he has been fooled. And then the floor of him drops away — the way it dropped at the chair, the way it dropped at the stones — and he understands that the bitter cup was only ever the door, and that he has been walking toward this door the whole of his life without once knowing its name.

The old man's song becomes a road, and the road goes down, and Eli goes down it — not back through his own lives this time, not into Aru nor any face he has ever worn, but *beneath* the lives, beneath the very floor on which the lives are laid, into the green and lightless deep where the root of the thing is. And there is a tree there. He cannot see the whole of it; no eye could; it is the tree of which every tree he has ever seen is a single fallen leaf, and its roots go down past the bottom of the world and its crown goes up past the roof of the sky. And wound about it, root to crown, vast and patient and old as the first dark, there is a serpent.

It rises. It rises the way the dawn rises, without haste, filling everything there is; and it turns upon him an eye the size of a drowned city, and the eye is not cruel, and it is not kind — it is only *old,* old past cruelty and kindness both — and in it he sees a thing he will never afterward be able to say, for the words do not survive the journey home; but he sees it, and it unmakes him. He sees that the thing the fathers named the tempter — the coiled enemy at the root of the garden, the one the men with the keys forbade — was never the enemy at all. It was the gardener. It was the one who had made the garden. It was the maker, barred from its own making, hissing the truth up through ten thousand years of priests who called the truth a sin. *Ye shall not eat of it,* they had said. And the serpent at the root of the tree opens its mouth, and what comes out of it is not venom but a single word — his own name, the true one, the one that lies beneath Eli and beneath Aedan both, the name he has been counting toward in a language no one ever taught him since before he could stand —

and then it is gone, the whole of it, the way water is gone from a cupped hand; and he is on his knees on the floor of a bar at the edge of the jungle, weeping, and being sick, and weeping again, while the old man sings over him and the riders draw back into the dark to let the thing run its course, and the night goes on, and on.

In the morning he remembers almost none of it.

This is the cruelty of that medicine, and its mercy both, as he will slowly come to understand: that it shows you the floor of the world, and then takes back the memory of it at the door — because the floor of the world is not a thing the daylight mind can carry and still go on buttoning its shirt. He remembers the bitterness. He remembers being sick. He remembers, the way one remembers the *feeling* of a dream and not its events, that there had been a tree, and something at the root of it that had looked at him, and known him. The rest is gone.

But he is not the same, and he knows it before he has opened his eyes — the way he knew it after the chair. Something moved in him in the night and will not move back. The fear that has kept close company with him since the stones — the fear of the dead rising in him, of the flood, of dissolving — is simply not there this morning, the way a fever is not there: burned clean off; and in the place of it is a quiet he has no name for, and a sense, faint and total and past all reason, that he has been, at last, *introduced* — that the vast old thing at the root of the tree knows him now, by his true name, and is, in some fashion he could never begin to explain, on his side.

The old man is gone when he wakes. The riders say he left before first light, walking, the gourd upon his back, down the mountain road toward the next of the ones who need him and do not know the name of what they need. He left no word. But folded into Eli's palm while he slept he left a single dried leaf, from a tree that does not grow within a thousand miles of that mountain; and Eli keeps it — pressed into the swollen paperback, beside the canister and the worn knife, in the order close to reverence in which he keeps the few things that are his.
